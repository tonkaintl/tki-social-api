    "draft_markdown": "A seller who won’t let the buyer write down the VIN is selling a problem. The VIN is a truck’s serial number; it’s how ownership, accident history, title brands, and recalls are traced. Skip that check and hidden damage, stolen-recovery flags, or a washed title can turn a work truck into a legal and financial headache.\n\nHow the problem shows up\n\nA typical scenario: the truck looks solid in the lot, the seller offers a quick cash deal, and paperwork looks thin. The buyer glances at the dash and sees a VIN, but the numbers on the title aren’t the same. That mismatch is a hard red flag. It often signals fraud, a rebuilt or salvage history, or errors that will block registration.\n\nWhat the VIN actually reveals\n\nThe VIN is a 17-character identifier required by federal regulation. It encodes manufacture details—country, manufacturer, vehicle type and engine options—that public decoders can read. The NHTSA’s public VIN decoder will return build plant and country information and accepts partial VINs for searches. Paid vehicle-history services pull the VIN into detailed reports that list accidents, ownership counts, service records, and recall status. Free checks exist too: the National Insurance Crime Bureau’s VINCheck will flag insurance theft claims and vehicles reported as salvage by participating insurers. NMVTIS aggregates title-brand and salvage data specifically to protect buyers from fraud and unsafe vehicles.\n\nWhere to look on the truck\n\nCommon VIN locations include the driver-side dashboard visible through the windshield, the driver-side doorjamb, and under the hood. Compare those physical VINs to the number on the title, bill of sale, and any maintenance records. If the numbers don’t match exactly, the sale should not proceed until the discrepancy is resolved.\n\nA practical verification flow\n\n- Step 1: Record the VIN from the dashboard, doorjamb, and under the hood. Note any signs of tampering: mismatched rivets, fresh paint around the plate, or different font styles.\n- Step 2: Compare the VIN to the title and bill of sale. Exact match only; transposed digits are a legal problem. If a partial VIN is offered, use NHTSA’s decoder to verify model year and build plant immediately.\n- Step 3: Run free checks. Use NICB’s VINCheck to screen for theft or salvage claims and query NMVTIS for title-brand history. These searches catch many obvious red flags without upfront cost.\n- Step 4: Purchase a paid history report (CARFAX, AutoCheck) when the truck clears the free checks. Paid reports typically show accident history, service records, previous owners, and whether the truck was branded salvage or rebuilt—information critical to estimating repair needs and resale value.\n- Step 5: Check recalls via NHTSA and verify any open recall work has been completed. Use Kelley Blue Book or AutoCheck tools to cross-check market value after adjusting for condition and title history.\n\nWhen to walk away or press the seller\n\nA mismatch between the vehicle’s VIN and title, a history showing salvage/rebuilt branding, or a NICB theft flag are non-starters for most buyers.
