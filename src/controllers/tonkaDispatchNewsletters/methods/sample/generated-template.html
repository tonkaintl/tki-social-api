More spill, less hydropower: The electric grid tradeoffs Washington can’t ignore

Renewable Energy World • Sean Wolfe • 05/11/2026

Federal court orders to increase Columbia River dam spill for salmon protection will reduce hydropower output by 1,000 megawatts in August and 500 in September, costing ratepayers over $100 million annually, at a time when grid reliability assessments show blackout risks across the Northwest through 2035. Energy modeling projects a 9-gigawatt shortfall in dependable electricity by 2030 and 14-18 gigawatts by 2035, driven by data center growth and electrification demands, making the loss of hydropower generation particularly problematic for grid stability. Without emerging clean energy technologies like small modular reactors or fusion, meeting Washington's climate mandates while replacing hydropower would nearly triple retail electricity rates, making hydropower the only currently available dispatchable resource capable of providing sustained output during peak demand periods.

I-70 truck parking project advances as drivers run out of space

Land Line • Land Line Media • 05/11/2026

Missouri has selected contractor KCI to lead a $30+ million truck parking expansion project along I-70, adding 255 spaces across rest areas in Concordia, Boonville, Mineola, and Wright City, with construction beginning fall 2026 through late 2028. The project addresses critical capacity shortages, with over 10,000 trucks daily using the corridor and both public and private parking at 75% overcapacity during peak hours. Spot rates rise, trucking jobs grow. Is the freight market turning around?

Land Line • Tyson Fisher • 05/11/2026

The trucking industry is showing signs of recovery as spot rates have risen consistently over six months and more than 4,000 trucking jobs were added in April—the largest monthly increase since September 2023. After four years of overcapacity and declining demand that eliminated approximately 123,000 jobs since October 2022, carriers are now adding capacity in preparation for what could be the strongest rate environment since the pandemic. While diesel price spikes pose a risk to profitability, industry analysts express cautious optimism about the upcycle, particularly in the open deck sector driven by data center construction and manufacturing improvements.
